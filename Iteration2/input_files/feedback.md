The current results demonstrate a successful implementation of a GPU-accelerated PM code, achieving the target 5% accuracy within the specified range. However, the analysis currently lacks a rigorous investigation into the *source* of the observed power suppression at $k > 0.22 \, h/\text{Mpc}$. Attributing this solely to "PM force resolution" is a qualitative assumption that requires quantitative verification to be scientifically robust.

**Critical Weaknesses & Gaps:**
1. **Resolution vs. Aliasing:** You have not decoupled the effects of the CIC window function, grid-aliasing, and the inherent PM force softening. The current $512^3$ mesh has a Nyquist frequency of $\approx 1.6 \, h/\text{Mpc}$, yet you see significant suppression starting at $k \approx 0.25 \, h/\text{Mpc}$. This is surprisingly early for a PM code.
2. **Missing Convergence Test:** The research plan mentioned a $1024^3$ resolution study, but it was not executed. Without this, you cannot distinguish between numerical artifacts (grid resolution) and physical limitations of the PM algorithm.
3. **Shot Noise Treatment:** You mention subtracting $1/\bar{n}$, but at $k > 0.2 \, h/\text{Mpc}$, the shot noise correction becomes a dominant term in the power spectrum. Any error in the estimation of the effective number density $\bar{n}$ or the assumption of Poissonian noise will manifest as a bias in the ratio.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Execute the $1024^3$ Resolution Study:** Run at least one realization at $1024^3$ mesh resolution (keeping $512^3$ particles). If the power suppression at $k \approx 0.25 \, h/\text{Mpc}$ persists, the issue is not grid resolution but rather the force interpolation scheme or the time-stepping integration error. If the power increases, you have quantified the resolution limit of your $512^3$ grid.
2. **Deconvolve the CIC Window:** Explicitly apply the inverse CIC window function $W^{-2}(k) = [\text{sinc}(k_x \Delta/2) \text{sinc}(k_y \Delta/2) \text{sinc}(k_z \Delta/2)]^{-2}$ to your measured $P(k)$ before comparing to the Quijote reference. This is standard practice and will clarify if the suppression is an artifact of the mass assignment method.
3. **Quantify the "Force Softening" Effect:** PM codes effectively have a force softening length related to the cell size. Compare your measured $P(k)$ against a theoretical PM-prediction (e.g., using a modified linear theory that incorporates the grid-based force smoothing) rather than the full non-linear Quijote spectrum. This will reveal if your code is performing as expected for a PM algorithm, even if it deviates from the "truth" (which includes higher-order non-linearities not captured by PM).
4. **Refine the $k < 0.03$ Analysis:** The high variance at low-$k$ is expected, but you should verify if the mean of your 10 realizations is biased by the specific realization of the large-scale modes. Check if the ratio $\langle P_{warp} \rangle / P_{quijote}$ at low-$k$ is consistent with the expected sample variance of a 1000 Mpc/h box.
